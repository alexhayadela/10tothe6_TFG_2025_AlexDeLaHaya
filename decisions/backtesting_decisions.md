## Part 1 -- Realistic Backtesting Framework

### 1.1 Lookahead Bias Prevention

Lookahead bias is the single most common and most devastating error in financial backtesting. A backtest that inadvertently uses future information will produce results that are literally impossible to achieve in live trading, and the inflation can be enormous -- a strategy that appears to deliver a Sharpe ratio of 2.0 in a contaminated backtest may deliver 0.3 or even negative returns in practice. The insidious nature of lookahead bias is that it can enter at multiple stages of the pipeline, and each leak may individually appear small while collectively rendering the entire exercise worthless.

The first and most obvious source of leakage is in feature calculation. Many of our technical indicators use rolling windows (e.g., SMA ratios, RSI, Bollinger bands), and a careless implementation might center these windows rather than trail them. Every feature computed for date t must use only data available at or before the close of day t. This sounds trivial but becomes subtle when dealing with features like `vol_ratio_5_20`, which compares a 5-day rolling volatility to a 20-day rolling volatility: if the rolling window is inclusive of day t, the feature implicitly contains day-t close information, which is acceptable only if we assume the signal is generated after the close (which we do -- see Section 1.3 on execution assumptions). However, any feature that uses day t+1 or later data, even indirectly through a centered moving average or a forward-filled missing value, constitutes a fatal leak. We recommend that all feature computation functions be audited with a simple test: compute features as of date t, then add one more day of data and recompute -- the features for date t must not change.

The second source of leakage is in label alignment. Our target variable `y_t` encodes whether the stock goes up over the next h days. Specifically, `y_t = 1` if the close on day t+h exceeds the close on day t. This means that for h=1, the label for day t depends on the close of day t+1, and for h=5, it depends on the close of day t+5. During training, labels are of course available (they are in the past relative to the test window), but the backtester must ensure that at prediction time t, it never has access to `y_t` or any feature derived from prices on days t+1 through t+h. A common subtle violation occurs when the scaler (StandardScaler or MinMaxScaler) is fit on the entire dataset including the test period. Even if the scaler does not directly reveal labels, it reveals distributional properties of future features (e.g., the mean and standard deviation of future RSI values), which can bias predictions. We enforce a strict rule: the scaler must be fit exclusively on training data and then applied (via `transform`, never `fit_transform`) to test data.

The third and most philosophically important protection against lookahead bias is the choice of evaluation protocol. Traditional k-fold cross-validation, where the dataset is randomly split into k folds regardless of temporal ordering, is fundamentally inappropriate for time series prediction. Roberts (1967) first identified the data snooping problem in financial forecasting, noting that random partitioning of time series data violates the independence assumption and allows the model to "see" the future during training. In k-fold CV applied to our dataset, fold 3 might contain data from 2018 while fold 2 contains data from 2019, meaning the model trained on folds 1-2-4-5 has literally trained on future data relative to the test fold. The autocorrelation in financial returns and the overlap in rolling-window features amplify this leakage dramatically.

Our chosen protocol is walk-forward (rolling out-of-sample) evaluation, which we implement in two variants. The sliding window variant trains on a fixed-length window of W years and tests on the subsequent period (typically 1 year), then slides forward. The expanding window variant uses all available data up to the test period. Both variants guarantee that no future data contaminates training. The sliding window has the advantage of adapting to regime changes (old data may be harmful if the market structure has changed), while the expanding window has the advantage of maximizing training data. Lopez de Prado (2018, Ch. 7) introduces purged k-fold cross-validation as a middle ground that respects temporal ordering while allowing multiple test folds, and we use this within each training window for hyperparameter tuning. However, the final out-of-sample evaluation must be strictly walk-forward.

Bailey, Borwein, Lopez de Prado, and Zhu (2016) formalize the probability of backtest overfitting (PBO), demonstrating that even with proper walk-forward evaluation, the researcher can overfit by running many backtests and selecting the best one. Their Combinatorially Symmetric Cross-Validation (CSCV) framework estimates the probability that an in-sample optimal strategy will underperform the median out-of-sample. We address this concern in Part 3, but note here that the first line of defense is limiting the number of researcher degrees of freedom during the backtesting phase.

### 1.2 Transaction Costs and Market Impact

A backtest without transaction costs is a fantasy. The academic literature is littered with strategies that generate spectacular paper returns but collapse under realistic friction assumptions. For IBEX35 stocks, we must account for three distinct cost components: the bid-ask spread, the broker commission, and market impact.

The bid-ask spread for IBEX35 constituents varies substantially by market capitalization and liquidity. The five most liquid names (Santander, BBVA, Inditex, Iberdrola, Telefonica) typically trade with spreads of 2-5 basis points during regular hours. Mid-cap constituents (Bankinter, Merlin Properties, CIE Automotive, Sacyr) can exhibit spreads of 8-15 basis points, particularly during the opening auction and near the close. For less liquid names or during volatile periods, spreads can widen to 20-30 basis points. We recommend using a tiered spread assumption: 5 basis points for the top-10 by market cap, 10 basis points for the next 10, and 15 basis points for the bottom 10. This is conservative but realistic. Korajczyk and Sadka (2004) demonstrate that transaction costs are the primary reason that many documented anomalies do not survive in practice, particularly for strategies that trade frequently in mid-cap stocks.

Broker commissions for European equities have declined substantially over the past decade due to MiFID II and competitive pressure from low-cost brokers. A realistic assumption for an institutional or semi-institutional setup is 3-5 basis points per trade (round trip: 6-10 basis points). For a retail investor using a discount broker, commissions may be as low as a flat fee per trade, but the percentage impact depends on position size. We assume 5 basis points per side (10 basis points round trip) as a baseline.

Market impact is the most difficult cost to estimate and is often ignored in academic backtests. When we trade all 30 IBEX35 stocks simultaneously on a given day, even with modest position sizes, we are collectively moving correlated assets in the same direction. Frazzini, Israel, and Moskowitz (2018) provide a comprehensive analysis of trading costs for institutional equity strategies, finding that market impact is the dominant cost component for any strategy that trades more than a few million euros per day. For our purposes, assuming relatively small position sizes (under 100,000 euros per stock per trade), direct market impact is negligible. However, if this strategy were scaled to institutional size, the impact on mid-cap IBEX35 names would become material, and a square-root impact model (Almgren & Chriss, 2001) should be incorporated.

The combined cost assumption for the backtest is: 10 basis points per side for the top 10 names (5 bps spread + 5 bps commission), 15 basis points for the next 10, and 20 basis points for the bottom 10. For a round trip (buy then sell, or sell short then cover), this means 20-40 basis points. At h=1, with daily rebalancing, a strategy that turns over 100% of the portfolio daily would incur approximately 20-40 basis points per day, or roughly 50-100% annually in transaction costs alone. This sets a brutally high bar: the model must generate at least 50-100 basis points per day of gross alpha to break even. At h=5, with weekly rebalancing, costs are reduced by a factor of 5, making the hurdle much more achievable.

### 1.3 Execution Assumptions

The timing of trade execution relative to signal generation is a critical design decision that directly affects both backtest realism and performance. We adopt the following convention, which we refer to as T+1 execution at the open.

The signal generation process is as follows: at the close of day t, all price and volume data for day t become available. Features for day t are computed, the model produces a prediction (probability of up for each of the 30 stocks), and the signal is generated. No action is taken on day t because the market has already closed. On the morning of day t+1, before the market opens, we place orders to execute at the opening auction or as close to the open as possible. This means the effective entry price is the open of day t+1, not the close of day t.

Why not execute at the close of day t? Because the signal requires the close price as an input (through features like close-to-close returns, RSI, etc.), so the signal is not available until after the close. Executing at the close would require submitting orders during the closing auction based on incomplete information (the close price is not yet determined), which introduces execution risk and potential lookahead. Some practitioners use a "close-to-close" convention where the signal is computed using the close of day t-1 (yesterday), and execution occurs at the close of day t. This avoids the timing issue but introduces a full day of signal decay, which is particularly costly for h=1 predictions where the informational content of the signal is already short-lived.

We recommend T+1 open execution for two reasons. First, it is unambiguously feasible: the trader has the full overnight period to compute signals, review positions, and place orders for the opening auction. Second, the opening auction of the Bolsa de Madrid is liquid and transparent, with a well-defined price discovery mechanism. The opening price is typically within 10-20 basis points of the previous close for liquid names, so the execution slippage between the theoretical close-to-close return and the realized open-to-open return is manageable.

For the return calculation in the backtest, we use: `return_t = (open_{t+2} / open_{t+1}) - 1` for h=1 (enter at open of t+1, exit at open of t+2), and `return_t = (open_{t+6} / open_{t+1}) - 1` for h=5 (enter at open of t+1, exit at open of t+6). This is a deliberate choice to avoid using any close prices in the return calculation, which eliminates any ambiguity about whether we could have executed at those prices.

Short selling on the Spanish market is subject to specific regulatory constraints. The CNMV (Comision Nacional del Mercado de Valores) requires disclosure of net short positions exceeding 0.10% of issued share capital and public disclosure at 0.50%. More practically, short selling requires borrowing shares from a broker, which incurs a borrowing fee of typically 25-75 basis points annualized for liquid IBEX35 names, though this can spike to several percent for heavily shorted or illiquid names. Additionally, Spain has historically imposed temporary short-selling bans during market crises (most recently during COVID-19 in March 2020), which would leave a short strategy unable to establish new positions precisely when the signal is most likely to predict down.

For position sizing, we consider three alternatives. Equal-weight allocation distributes capital equally among all stocks with a signal (after confidence filtering), which maximizes simplicity and diversification. Confidence-weighted allocation sizes positions proportional to |P(up) - 0.5|, which allocates more capital to higher-conviction predictions; this approach is natural given the confidence analysis in `notebooks/confidence_analysis.ipynb` and provides a direct translation of model output to portfolio weight. The Kelly criterion (Kelly, 1956) provides the theoretically optimal bet size as `f* = (bp - q) / b`, where p is the probability of winning, q = 1-p, and b is the payoff ratio. For a binary direction bet, `f* = 2p - 1`, which for p=0.54 gives f*=0.08 (risk 8% of capital per bet). However, full Kelly is known to be extremely aggressive and produces large drawdowns; practitioners universally recommend fractional Kelly (typically quarter-Kelly or half-Kelly). We recommend equal-weight allocation for the primary backtest, with confidence-weighted as a sensitivity analysis. Kelly-based sizing is theoretically elegant but requires accurate probability calibration, which our models may not deliver (see the calibration analysis in the confidence notebook).

### 1.4 Benchmark and Metrics

A backtest is meaningless without a benchmark, and the choice of benchmark determines whether the strategy adds value. We propose four benchmarks, ordered from most to least permissive.

The first benchmark is the buy-and-hold IBEX35 index. This represents the simplest equity exposure to the Spanish market. Over the typical backtest period (2020-2025), the IBEX35 has delivered annualized returns of approximately 5-8% with volatility of 18-22%, yielding a Sharpe ratio of 0.25-0.40. Any active strategy must substantially exceed this Sharpe to justify its complexity.

The second benchmark is a random long strategy: on each signal day, randomly select half the stocks to go long (equal weight) and hold cash or go neutral on the rest. This controls for the general equity risk premium while introducing the same level of concentration as our strategy. By running 1,000 bootstrap samples of the random strategy, we can construct a distribution of Sharpe ratios and returns, against which our strategy's realized Sharpe can be compared.

The third benchmark is a 50/50 position: always predict each stock as up with 50% probability. This produces a portfolio that is equally long and short all 30 stocks (market-neutral). By construction, its expected return is zero (minus transaction costs), and its realized return reflects only random fluctuations. This is the null hypothesis: if our strategy cannot beat a coin flip after costs, the model has no predictive power.

The fourth benchmark is a "naive momentum" strategy: predict up if the stock's return over the past h days was positive. This is a simple, well-documented anomaly (Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993) that requires no ML. If our sophisticated models cannot beat this rule-of-thumb, the added complexity is not justified.

For return metrics, we report: annualized Sharpe ratio (using excess returns over the risk-free rate, which for EUR has been approximately 3-4% in 2025-2026), maximum drawdown (the largest peak-to-trough decline in cumulative returns, which captures tail risk far better than Sharpe alone), Calmar ratio (annualized return divided by maximum drawdown, which penalizes strategies with large drawdowns even if average returns are high), and CAGR (compound annual growth rate, the geometric mean return).

Statistical significance is essential and almost universally neglected in backtest reporting. A strategy that produces a Sharpe ratio of 0.8 over 3 years may well be indistinguishable from noise. The minimum backtest length required to distinguish a true Sharpe of S from zero at the 95% confidence level is approximately `T = (2/S)^2` years. For S=0.5 (a respectable Sharpe), this requires T=16 years; for S=1.0, T=4 years. With a 3-year out-of-sample period, we can only reliably detect Sharpe ratios above approximately 1.15. Bailey and Lopez de Prado (2014) introduce the deflated Sharpe ratio (DSR), which adjusts the observed Sharpe for the number of trials (strategies tested), non-normality of returns (skewness and kurtosis), and the length of the backtest. The DSR provides a p-value for the hypothesis that the true Sharpe is positive, accounting for multiple testing. We recommend reporting the DSR alongside the raw Sharpe.

A critical conceptual point: balanced accuracy in cross-validation (the primary metric used in model training and selection) does not directly translate to Sharpe ratio in the backtest. There are several reasons for this disconnect. First, balanced accuracy treats all correct predictions equally, but the economic value of a correct prediction depends on the magnitude of the subsequent return. Predicting "up" correctly on a day when the stock rises 0.01% is economically worthless, while predicting "up" on a day when it rises 3% is extremely valuable. Second, balanced accuracy is unconditional -- it averages over all market conditions -- while Sharpe ratio is path-dependent and heavily influenced by the timing of correct and incorrect predictions. A model that is correct during calm markets but wrong during crises will have decent balanced accuracy but terrible Sharpe (because crisis periods have the largest returns and thus the largest impact on cumulative P&L). Third, balanced accuracy ignores transaction costs, while Sharpe ratio is computed on net-of-cost returns. This disconnect motivates the backtest as a separate and essential evaluation step beyond CV metrics.

## Part 2 -- Strategy Design: All Stocks vs. High-Confidence Filter

### 2.1 Baseline: Trade All Predictions

The simplest strategy is to take a position on every stock for which the model produces a prediction. On each signal day, we go long all stocks for which P(up) >= 0.5 and short (or hold cash in the long-only variant) all stocks for which P(up) < 0.5. This is the "trade everything" approach, corresponding to a confidence threshold of tau = 0.50 in the notation of the confidence analysis notebook.

This baseline has two important properties. First, it maximizes diversification: on any given day, we hold positions in all 30 IBEX35 stocks, which provides substantial cross-sectional risk reduction. The idiosyncratic risk of any single stock is diversified away, and the portfolio's return variance is dominated by the common factor (market beta). Second, it minimizes selection bias: we are not cherry-picking which predictions to act on, which eliminates one source of researcher degrees of freedom. If the model has any edge at all, trading all predictions will capture it, diluted by the noise from low-confidence predictions.

The downside is clear: many of these predictions are essentially coin flips. A stock with P(up) = 0.501 is barely distinguishable from random, yet the baseline strategy treats it identically to a stock with P(up) = 0.65. The confidence analysis in `notebooks/confidence_analysis.ipynb` quantifies this directly: at tau = 0.50 (all predictions), coverage is 100% and balanced accuracy is at the baseline level. The noise from near-0.50 predictions dilutes the signal from high-confidence predictions, reducing the overall information ratio of the portfolio.

From a practical standpoint, the all-predictions baseline also maximizes turnover and transaction costs. With h=1, the model may flip its prediction on a given stock from "up" to "down" across consecutive days based on minor feature changes, generating a round trip that incurs 20-40 basis points for no informational benefit. This churning is most severe for stocks with predicted probabilities near 0.50, where small perturbations cause the hard prediction to oscillate.

Despite these limitations, the all-predictions baseline serves an essential role in the backtesting framework. It is the strategy that most directly corresponds to the CV metrics we have already computed. If balanced accuracy in CV is 53.5%, the all-predictions backtest should produce returns broadly consistent with a 53.5% hit rate (adjusted for the magnitude distribution of returns and transaction costs). Any significant deviation between the CV-implied returns and the backtest returns indicates a problem with either the backtest implementation or the CV protocol.

### 2.2 Confidence-Filtered Strategy

The confidence-filtered strategy acts only on predictions where the model is sufficiently certain. Specifically, we enter a position on stock i at time t only if |P(up)_i,t - 0.5| > delta, where delta corresponds to a confidence threshold tau = 0.5 + delta. In the notation of the confidence analysis notebook, this means we keep predictions where P(up) > tau or P(up) < 1 - tau, and abstain on the rest.

The theoretical motivation for confidence filtering is straightforward: if the model's predicted probabilities are well-calibrated, then predictions with P(up) = 0.65 should have a 65% chance of being correct, while predictions with P(up) = 0.51 should have only a 51% chance. By filtering out the latter, we improve the average quality of our trading signals at the cost of reduced coverage (fewer positions). This is exactly the accuracy-coverage tradeoff analyzed in section B3 of the confidence notebook.

The empirical results from the confidence analysis provide direct guidance. Sweeping tau from 0.50 to 0.75 reveals whether higher confidence thresholds yield meaningfully better balanced accuracy on held-out data. The critical question is whether the improvement in accuracy is large enough to compensate for the reduction in coverage and the resulting loss of diversification. If, for example, increasing tau from 0.50 to 0.60 improves balanced accuracy from 53.5% to 56.0% while reducing coverage from 100% to 60%, the filtered strategy may be superior because the improved hit rate more than offsets the concentration risk. Conversely, if accuracy improves only to 54.0% while coverage drops to 40%, the filter is not worth the cost.

Position sizing within the filtered portfolio presents two natural options. Equal-weight allocation divides capital equally among all stocks that pass the confidence threshold, regardless of their predicted probability. This is simple and avoids amplifying calibration errors: if the model systematically overestimates probabilities for certain stocks, equal-weight prevents excessive allocation to those stocks. Probability-proportional allocation, where the weight of stock i is proportional to |P(up)_i - 0.5|, allocates more capital to higher-conviction predictions. This is the natural extension of the confidence filter and is theoretically optimal if probabilities are well-calibrated. The calibration analysis (reliability diagrams) in the confidence notebook directly informs this choice: if the calibration curves are close to the diagonal, probability-proportional weighting is justified; if they show systematic over- or under-confidence, equal-weight is safer.

Jegadeesh and Titman (1993), in their seminal study of momentum strategies, demonstrate the value of signal filtering: sorting stocks into deciles by past return and trading only the extreme deciles (top and bottom) produces higher risk-adjusted returns than trading the full cross-section. Avramov, Chordia, Jostova, and Philipov (2006) extend this insight to stock selection based on predictive models, showing that concentrated portfolios of high-conviction picks outperform broad portfolios when the underlying signal has genuine predictive power. Our confidence filter applies the same logic to model-based signals.

A practical consideration is the minimum number of positions required for adequate diversification. With 30 IBEX35 stocks, a threshold that keeps only 5-6 stocks per signal day creates substantial concentration risk: a single earnings surprise or sector shock can dominate the portfolio's return. We recommend requiring a minimum of 8-10 positions after filtering. If the threshold tau produces fewer than this minimum on a given day, we have two options: lower the threshold for that day to include more stocks (adaptive threshold), or skip that day entirely (sit in cash). The latter is cleaner from a methodology standpoint but introduces periods of no exposure, which complicates return calculations.

The confidence threshold tau should be treated as a hyperparameter, selected based on in-sample cross-validation results and fixed before running the out-of-sample backtest. The confidence analysis notebook provides the accuracy-coverage curves that inform this selection. Crucially, tau must never be selected by maximizing backtest Sharpe ratio, as this would constitute data snooping of the worst kind -- the entire backtest becomes overfit to a single parameter choice. Section 4.3 discusses this integration in detail.

### 2.3 Long-Only vs. Long/Short

The choice between long-only and long/short execution has profound implications for the strategy's risk profile, implementability, and return expectations. We analyze three variants.

The long-only variant takes a long position in all stocks predicted to go up (with P(up) > tau) and holds cash for stocks predicted to go down or those that do not pass the confidence filter. This is the most realistic variant for retail investors and pension funds, which typically face restrictions on short selling. The long-only strategy has inherent market exposure (positive beta): on average, roughly half the IBEX35 stocks will be predicted as up on any given day, so the strategy is approximately 50% invested (varying with the number of up predictions and the confidence filter). The returns of this strategy will be positively correlated with the IBEX35, and the alpha (if any) will be embedded within substantial market risk. In a prolonged bear market, the long-only strategy will lose money even if every prediction is correct, simply because the long positions decline.

The long/short variant goes long stocks predicted up and short stocks predicted down. In the equal-weight case, this produces a portfolio that is approximately market-neutral (equal dollar amounts long and short), with returns driven primarily by the spread between winners and losers. This is theoretically cleaner for alpha extraction: if the model correctly identifies stocks that will outperform and underperform, the long/short spread captures this alpha regardless of the market direction. However, the practical challenges are considerable. Short selling on the Spanish market incurs borrowing costs (25-75 basis points annualized for liquid names, higher for illiquid ones), requires margin maintenance (which ties up capital), and is subject to regulatory risk (temporary bans during market stress). Furthermore, the short leg of the portfolio introduces convexity risk: a shorted stock can rise by more than 100%, while a long stock can only fall by 100%, creating an asymmetric risk profile.

The hedged long/short variant combines long positions in predicted-up stocks with a short position in the IBEX35 ETF (or futures) as a market hedge, rather than shorting individual stocks predicted to go down. This simplifies the short side (one instrument instead of 15), reduces borrowing costs and regulatory risk, and provides a clean beta hedge. The cost is that we lose the alpha from the short side: the model's predictions about which stocks will go down are not utilized. If the model is equally good at predicting up and down, this is a substantial loss. If the model is better at predicting up than down (which is common due to the slight majority-class bias), the loss is smaller.

We recommend running all three variants in the backtest to understand the source of returns. If the long-only strategy produces significant alpha (measured by Jensen's alpha relative to the IBEX35), the model is adding value on the long side. If the long/short strategy produces higher Sharpe than the long-only, the short side contributes incremental alpha. If the hedged variant produces similar Sharpe to the full long/short, the down-predictions are not adding information beyond the market hedge.

### 2.4 Horizon Considerations

The prediction horizon h directly determines the strategy's turnover, transaction cost burden, and the nature of the signal being exploited. The two horizons in our framework (h=1 and h=5) represent very different regimes.

At h=1 (one trading day), the signal changes daily, creating the potential for daily rebalancing of the entire portfolio. In the worst case, every stock's prediction flips from one day to the next, requiring 30 round trips per day. In practice, predictions exhibit some persistence (the features are autocorrelated, so the model's output is autocorrelated), but the turnover will still be high -- typically 30-60% of positions change on any given day. With round-trip transaction costs of 20-40 basis points per stock, daily turnover of 30-60% of 30 stocks generates daily costs of roughly 2-7 basis points on the portfolio level, or 5-18% annualized. This means the model must generate a gross alpha of at least 5-18% per year (equivalently, a balanced accuracy edge of roughly 2-4 percentage points over 50%) just to cover transaction costs. This is a formidable hurdle. Krauss, Do, and Huck (2017) find that daily trading strategies based on ML classifiers applied to S&P 500 stocks generate gross returns that are often consumed by transaction costs, leaving net returns that are marginal at best.

At h=5 (five trading days), the signal is rebalanced weekly. Turnover drops by approximately a factor of 5, and the transaction cost burden drops proportionally to 1-4% annualized. The hurdle for profitability is substantially lower, and a model with even a modest edge (1-2 percentage points over 50% balanced accuracy) may generate positive net returns. However, the h=5 signal raises its own challenges. First, the signal has more time to decay: the model predicts the direction over the next 5 days, but market conditions may change within that window (e.g., an earnings announcement on day 3 of the holding period). Second, the overlapping nature of 5-day returns creates statistical complications: returns for consecutive signal days overlap by 4 days, which means they are highly correlated. This overlap inflates the apparent Sharpe ratio and must be corrected using methods discussed in Section 3.4.

We recommend treating h=1 and h=5 as separate strategies with separate backtests. The h=1 strategy should be evaluated primarily on its ability to survive transaction costs, while the h=5 strategy should be evaluated on the decay and overlap issues. A hybrid approach -- using h=1 signals but rebalancing only when the signal changes by more than some threshold -- can reduce turnover while retaining the h=1 model's faster information incorporation. This "signal change filter" is distinct from the confidence filter and can be layered on top of it.

## Part 3 -- Common Backtesting Pitfalls

### 3.1 Multiple Testing and Overfitting

The multiple testing problem is arguably the most important unsolved issue in quantitative finance research. Harvey, Liu, and Zhu (2016) demonstrate that the majority of published financial "factors" are likely false discoveries, driven by the collective data mining of the academic community. The same risk applies to our backtesting exercise: we are evaluating six models (RF, XGBoost, GRU, LSTM, CNN+GRU, CNN+LSTM), two horizons (h=1 and h=5), multiple feature sets (micro, cross, macro), multiple confidence thresholds, and multiple execution variants. The total number of implicit strategies tested is easily in the dozens or hundreds, and if we report the best-performing combination, we are almost certainly reporting an overfit result.

Bailey et al. (2016) formalize this problem through the Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO). Their CSCV (Combinatorially Symmetric Cross-Validation) framework works as follows: given N walk-forward windows, generate all C(N, N/2) combinations of in-sample and out-of-sample subsets, select the best strategy in each in-sample subset, and evaluate it on the corresponding out-of-sample subset. The PBO is the fraction of combinations where the in-sample optimal strategy underperforms the median out-of-sample. A PBO above 50% indicates that the strategy selection process is no better than random. We recommend implementing CSCV as a final diagnostic after the primary backtest is complete.

The "researcher degrees of freedom" problem (Simmons, Nelson, & Simonsohn, 2011) refers to the many small decisions a researcher makes during the analysis that collectively inflate the false discovery rate. In our context, these include: which features to include, how to handle missing data, which rolling window length to use for CV, how to preprocess the data (scaling, outlier treatment), and which metrics to optimize. Each decision is a fork in the garden of forking paths, and the final reported result represents one specific path that may not generalize. Our mitigation strategy is threefold. First, we fix all preprocessing decisions before running the backtest (they were determined during the model development phase). Second, we use cross-validation for hyperparameter selection (not the backtest itself), which insulates the backtest from parameter optimization. Third, we report results for all model/horizon/variant combinations, not just the best one.

Crucially, the use of walk-forward CV for model selection partially mitigates the overfitting risk. Unlike a grid search over backtest parameters (which directly optimizes the quantity we are trying to measure), CV selects model parameters on data that is temporally prior to the backtest period. The backtest then provides a genuinely out-of-sample evaluation. However, this protection is weakened if we iterate: running the backtest, observing disappointing results, returning to the model development phase to "improve" features or parameters, and re-running the backtest. Each iteration consumes some of the backtest's informational value. We recommend limiting ourselves to a maximum of two iterations and formally documenting any post-hoc changes.

### 3.2 Regime Sensitivity

Financial markets are non-stationary. The statistical properties of returns -- means, variances, correlations, and the relationships between features and outcomes -- change over time in ways that are partially predictable (business cycles, central bank policy regimes) and partially unpredictable (black swan events). An ML model trained on one regime may perform well in similar regimes and poorly in different ones. This is not a failure of the model per se -- it reflects the fundamental challenge of prediction in a non-stationary environment.

Our training data, depending on the window configuration, spans approximately 2015-2023. This period includes the European sovereign debt aftermath (2015-2016), the low-volatility rally (2017), the Q4 2018 selloff, the COVID-19 crash and recovery (2020), the post-pandemic inflation regime (2021-2022), and the monetary tightening cycle (2022-2023). However, it does not include the Global Financial Crisis of 2008-2009, which was a qualitatively different regime characterized by systemic banking risk, extreme correlations, and unprecedented policy interventions. If the out-of-sample backtest period (2023-2026) includes a similar crisis, the model has no analogous training data to draw on.

We recommend explicitly segmenting the backtest period into regimes and reporting performance separately for each. A simple approach uses the VIX (which is one of our macro features) as a regime indicator: low-volatility (VIX < 15), moderate-volatility (15 < VIX < 25), high-volatility (VIX > 25), and crisis (VIX > 35). Alternatively, we can use the IBEX35 return over the trailing 60 days to classify regimes as bullish (positive return, low vol), bearish (negative return, high vol), or transitional. The model's balanced accuracy and Sharpe ratio should be reported for each regime. If the model performs well in calm markets but poorly in volatile ones, this is a serious concern because volatile periods contribute disproportionately to the strategy's total return variance. Conversely, if the model performs well in volatile markets (where return magnitudes are large), the strategy may be robust despite mediocre performance in calm markets.

The expanding window training scheme has an advantage here: as new regimes are experienced, they enter the training set, and the model (when retrained) adapts. The sliding window scheme is more vulnerable because old regimes fall out of the training window. If the sliding window is 3 years and the model is being evaluated in 2026, it has no exposure to pre-2023 data. This means the COVID crash, which may contain useful information about crisis behavior, is no longer in the training set. We recommend using an expanding window for the primary backtest, with a sliding window as a sensitivity analysis.

### 3.3 Survivorship Bias

Survivorship bias arises when the backtest universe includes only stocks that survived (remained in the index) throughout the evaluation period, excluding stocks that were delisted, acquired, or dropped from the index. This creates an upward bias because removed stocks often underperformed (they were dropped due to declining market cap or financial distress), while new additions often outperformed (they were added after a period of strong performance).

The IBEX35 index is rebalanced semi-annually (in June and December) by the Bolsa de Madrid's Technical Advisory Committee. Over a typical 5-year period, several stocks enter and exit the index. Using the current 30 constituents for a backtest spanning 2020-2025 introduces survivorship bias because we are trading a universe that was selected, in part, based on information not available at the start of the backtest period.

The theoretically correct approach is to use point-in-time index membership: at each date in the backtest, trade only the stocks that were in the IBEX35 on that date. This requires historical constituent data, which is available from commercial data providers (Bloomberg, Refinitiv) and from the Bolsa de Madrid's public records. If point-in-time membership data is not available, a pragmatic alternative is to use the current 30 constituents but exclude any stock that was added to the index after the start of the backtest period. This is not perfect (it still excludes stocks that were removed) but eliminates the worst of the bias.

For our project, the impact of survivorship bias is partially mitigated by two factors. First, the IBEX35 is a relatively stable index -- turnover is lower than for broader indices like the Russell 2000. Second, our models predict direction (up/down), not magnitude, so the bias from excluding poorly performing delisted stocks is less severe than for a return-based strategy. Nevertheless, we recommend acknowledging this limitation and, if feasible, using point-in-time constituents.

### 3.4 Short-Horizon Autocorrelation and Overlapping Returns

At h=1, the model's predicted probability for stock i on day t is a function of features that are themselves functions of recent prices and volumes. Since these features use rolling windows (e.g., 5-day, 10-day, 20-day), the features for day t and day t+1 are highly correlated (they share most of their inputs). Consequently, the predicted probabilities are autocorrelated: P(up)_{i,t} and P(up)_{i,t+1} are positively correlated, often strongly so. This autocorrelation has two important consequences for the backtest.

First, positions are persistent. If the model predicts stock i as "up" today, it is likely to predict "up" tomorrow as well (because the features have not changed much). This means the strategy does not actually turn over 100% of positions daily -- the effective holding period is longer than one day, which reduces transaction costs below the worst-case estimate. This is beneficial for the strategy but means that the number of independent bets per year is much lower than 252 (the number of trading days). The effective number of independent observations is approximately `N_eff = N / (1 + 2 * sum(autocorrelation_k))` for k = 1, 2, ..., where the sum converges if the autocorrelation decays. For typical feature autocorrelations in our setting, the effective number of independent daily observations is roughly 60-100 per year, compared to 252 nominal observations.

Second, and more critically for statistical inference, the autocorrelation in returns and P&L inflates the apparent Sharpe ratio. The standard Sharpe ratio formula assumes independent returns, and when returns are positively autocorrelated (which they will be because positions are persistent), the estimated standard deviation of the mean return is biased downward, making Sharpe appear higher than it truly is. Lo (2002) provides the correction factor: the annualized Sharpe ratio should be deflated by a factor of sqrt((1 + 2 * sum(rho_k)) / q) where rho_k are the return autocorrelations and q is the number of observations per year. For moderately autocorrelated daily returns with effective autocorrelation of 0.3, this correction can reduce the apparent Sharpe by 20-30%.

At h=5, the problem is more severe because the 5-day holding periods overlap: the return from day t to day t+5 shares 4 days with the return from day t+1 to day t+6. This creates a moving-average structure in the return series that mechanically induces autocorrelation of approximately (h-1)/h at lag 1, decaying linearly to zero at lag h. The resulting Sharpe inflation is substantial and well-documented in the literature on overlapping returns (Richardson & Stock, 1989).

To address this, we recommend two complementary approaches. First, use Newey-West standard errors (Newey & West, 1987) with lag h when computing the statistical significance of mean returns. Newey-West adjusts the variance estimate for autocorrelation up to the specified lag, providing consistent standard errors even with overlapping returns. Second, use block bootstrap (Politis & Romano, 1994) to construct confidence intervals for the Sharpe ratio: resample the return series in blocks of length 2h (to preserve the autocorrelation structure), compute the Sharpe ratio for each bootstrap sample, and report the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles as the confidence interval. If the 2.5th percentile of the bootstrapped Sharpe is below zero, the strategy's apparent profitability is not statistically significant.

## Part 4 -- Implementation Roadmap

### 4.1 Data Requirements

The backtest requires several data components beyond what is used in model training. First, daily OHLCV data for all 30 IBEX35 stocks must extend through the full backtest period, including the out-of-sample evaluation window. The model training pipeline uses data up to the training cutoff, but the backtest requires prices for the subsequent period. Specifically, we need open prices for execution (T+1 open execution) and close prices for feature computation. This data must be split-adjusted and dividend-adjusted to avoid spurious returns around corporate actions.

Second, the IBEX35 index level is required for benchmarking (buy-and-hold comparison) and for the hedged long/short variant (shorting the index ETF). The most liquid IBEX35 ETF is the Lyxor IBEX 35 (MC.PA) or the Accion IBEX 35 ETF (BBVAI.MC). Index futures on BME (Mini IBEX 35) are an alternative for the hedge.

Third, a transaction cost schedule must be specified for the broker through which the strategy would be executed. As discussed in Section 1.2, we use tiered cost assumptions based on market capitalization, but these should be calibrated against actual broker quotes if available.

Fourth, for the survivorship bias analysis (Section 3.3), historical IBEX35 constituent data is needed. At minimum, the dates on which each stock entered and exited the index should be recorded.

Fifth, macro data (VIX, S&P 500 returns) must be available with the same frequency and lag assumptions as used in the features. Since VIX is computed from US options markets, which close after the European close, we use the previous day's VIX value to avoid lookahead (this is already handled in the feature pipeline).

### 4.2 Proposed Backtest Structure

The backtest proceeds in a walk-forward loop. We reserve the last 3 years of available data (approximately 2023-2026) as the out-of-sample backtest period. The model is trained on data prior to this period using the sliding or expanding window CV scheme established during model development. The loop operates as follows.

At the close of each trading day t in the backtest period, features are computed for all 30 stocks using data up to and including day t. The trained model produces predicted probabilities P(up)_i for each stock i. The confidence filter is applied: stocks with |P(up)_i - 0.5| < delta are excluded (where delta = tau - 0.5 and tau is the pre-selected confidence threshold). For the remaining stocks, positions are sized (equal-weight or confidence-weighted) and recorded. On the open of day t+1, the positions are executed at the opening price. The portfolio return for day t+1 is computed based on the open-to-open return from day t+1 to day t+2 (for h=1), net of transaction costs.

At periodic intervals (e.g., every 250 trading days), the model is retrained on all data available up to that point. The retrained model replaces the previous one for subsequent predictions. This periodic retraining captures any regime changes or structural breaks in the feature-return relationship. Between retraining dates, the model artifact is fixed, ensuring that the same model generates all predictions within a given evaluation window.

The backtest logs the following for each day: the date, the model used (which retrained version), the predicted probabilities for all 30 stocks, the confidence filter result (which stocks pass), the position weights, the execution prices, the transaction costs, and the net portfolio return. This granular log enables post-hoc analysis of when and why the strategy performs well or poorly.

The implementation should be modular, with separate components for signal generation (model prediction + confidence filter), portfolio construction (position sizing), execution simulation (price lookup + cost deduction), and performance measurement (return and risk metrics). This modularity allows sensitivity analysis on individual components -- for example, testing different cost assumptions without re-running the signal generation step.

### 4.3 Integration with Confidence Analysis

The confidence analysis notebook (`notebooks/confidence_analysis.ipynb`) provides the empirical basis for selecting the confidence threshold tau. The integration between the confidence analysis and the backtest must be handled carefully to avoid data snooping.

The correct procedure is as follows. During the model development phase (prior to the backtest), the confidence analysis is run on the cross-validation results. The accuracy-coverage curves from section B3 of the notebook reveal how balanced accuracy and coverage change as tau varies from 0.50 to 0.75. Based on these in-sample results, a threshold tau is selected. The selection criterion should balance accuracy improvement against coverage loss, with explicit consideration of the minimum number of positions required for diversification (Section 2.2). Once tau is selected, it is fixed and applied unchanged to the out-of-sample backtest period.

The crucial discipline is that tau must never be selected or adjusted based on backtest results. If we run the backtest with tau = 0.55 and observe a disappointing Sharpe ratio, then try tau = 0.60 and observe a better one, we have snooped on the backtest data and the improved Sharpe is unreliable. This is exactly the multiple testing problem described in Section 3.1, applied to a single parameter. If we wish to evaluate multiple thresholds, we must either report all of them (not just the best) or use the deflated Sharpe ratio to adjust for the number of thresholds tested.

The calibration analysis (reliability diagrams, Brier score, ECE) from section B2 of the confidence notebook informs whether probability-proportional position sizing is appropriate. If the model is well-calibrated (low ECE, calibration curve close to the diagonal), then the predicted probabilities are trustworthy measures of prediction quality, and sizing positions proportionally to |P(up) - 0.5| is well-motivated. If the model is poorly calibrated (high ECE, systematic over- or under-confidence), then the raw probabilities should not be used for sizing. In this case, either use equal-weight sizing or apply calibration post-processing (e.g., Platt scaling, isotonic regression) to the predicted probabilities before using them for sizing. However, any calibration method must be fit on the training data, not the backtest data.

The decisiveness analysis (Part A of the confidence notebook) provides an additional diagnostic. If windows where the model is more decisive (higher |mean_predicted_prob - 0.5|) also have higher balanced accuracy, this suggests that the model's confidence is informative -- it knows when it knows. This finding supports the confidence-filtered strategy: if the model can distinguish high-quality predictions from low-quality ones, filtering on confidence is rational. Conversely, if decisiveness and accuracy are uncorrelated, the model's confidence is uninformative, and filtering may simply reduce diversification without improving signal quality.
